Empowering Students to Protect and Conserve Endangered Species

Philippines' unique biodiversity and the importance of conservation.

The Philippines, an archipelago of more than 7,000 islands, is renowned for its remarkable biodiversity and rich natural resources. As one of the world's 17 megadiverse countries, the nation boasts an extraordinary variety of flora and fauna, many of which are endemic, meaning they are found nowhere else on Earth. This unique ecosystem is the result of the islands' geological history, tropical climate, and diverse habitats, ranging from lush rainforests and mangroves to coral reefs and mountains.

Unfortunately, the Philippines' incredible biodiversity is under threat due to factors such as deforestation, habitat loss, pollution, overexploitation of resources, and climate change. The rapid decline of many species has prompted urgent calls for increased conservation efforts to protect the country's natural heritage for future generations. Wildlife conservation is not only crucial for preserving the unique species found in the Philippines but also for maintaining the balance of ecosystems, which are essential for human survival and wellbeing. Healthy ecosystems provide vital services such as clean air, water purification, climate regulation, and food production.

The importance of wildlife conservation in the Philippines cannot be overstated. By working together to protect and conserve endangered and economically important species, we can help ensure the continued survival of these unique creatures and the ecosystems they inhabit. This, in turn, will benefit the people of the Philippines and contribute to the global effort to preserve our planet's natural treasures for generations to come.

List of Endangered Species in the Philippines

Philippine Eagle

The Philippine eagle, sometimes known as the monkey-eating eagle, is the country’s national bird and is one of many endemic species in the Philippines. It has a brown-and-white feather pattern and a bushy crest, and is thought to be one of the world’s largest and most powerful birds. Deforestation, mining, and pollution are all major risks to the Philippine eagle’s survival.

In 1990, the Philippine eagle was added to the red list of critically endangered wildlife created by the IUCN (International Union for Conservation of Nature).

There are only a few of these eagles left in the world. For the past 56 years, their global population has been steadily falling. Various laws have been created to protect the Philippine eagle, but they have been poorly enforced, resulting in the eagle’s continuous decline.

Natural parks such as Mt. Apo and Mt. Katinglad have natural reserves and other protected areas. The captive breeding of these eagles is overseen by the Philippine Eagle Center in Davao, on the island of Mindanao.

Tamaraw

The tamaraw, sometimes known as the dwarf forest buffalo, is an animal found only in the Philippines, specifically on the island of Mindoro.

During the 1900s, these endemic beasts roamed freely across Mindoro’s wide expanse, but as man began to develop and populate the island, the tamaraw population began to fall. When Mindoro was attacked by the rinderpest, or cow epidemic, in the 1930s, their numbers plummeted.

The tamaraws were also victims of illegal hunting and the animal flesh trade among Mindoro residents. These indigenous beasts were on the verge of extinction by the 1960s. In 1969, it was thought that less than 100 animals remained, placing the tamaraw on the IUCN’s critically endangered list.

5,000 hectares of Mount Iglit-Baco, in Mindoro, were turned into a National Park to provide a safe haven for tamaraws in order to encourage their conservation and protection. These unique beasts are free to roam and procreate in their native habitat. After successful conservation efforts, there are around 480 tamaraws in the wild today.

Philippine Crocodile

The IUCN has classified the Philippine freshwater crocodile, or Mindoro crocodile, as critically endangered. The decline of the Philippine freshwater crocodile has been attributed to illicit hunting and dynamite fishing.

The Philippine crocodile’s greatest threat is the enormous clearance of rainforests that serve as the crocodile’s natural home. The woodlands are being cleared to make way for farming.

Another threat comes from local people, who hunt the crocodiles illegally. Locals need to be educated about the differences between the small Philippine freshwater crocodile and the larger saltwater crocodiles that live in the same area. Locals frequently hunt the smaller, endangered native crocodile species without recognising the consequences.

Net Coral

The number of net corals is in decline. Due to this population trend, it has been classified as an endangered species by the IUCN.

This variety of coral has been targeted in the aquarium trade, as its appearance makes it a desirable addition to a tank. In addition to being extracted and used in aquariums, net corals are also vulnerable to coral bleaching. Net corals are strongly reactive to the bleaching phenomenon, making them a species that is likely to go extinct soon.

Walden’s Hornbill

Walden’s Hornbill is native to the Philippine islands of Panay and Negros; there are also small flocks in Zamboanga del Norte, Mindanao. Excessive hunting and illegal logging have resulted in the extinction of this species in the Negros and Guimaras provinces. As a result, it has been added to the IUCN list of critically endangered species.

Visayan Warty Pig

The IUCN lists the Visayan Warty Pig as a critically endangered species in the Philippines. The pig, which was once common throughout the central Philippines, is now only found on two islands: Panay and Negros. Although it has not been proved, there may still be a small herd on Masbate island.

Illegal hunting and logging, as well as agricultural land destruction, contributed to the Visayan Warty Pig’s extinction on Cebu. To meet the region’s increased need for crops, the animals’ natural habitats were converted into rice fields. This species is now bred in captivity in small numbers. Although they are extremely rare, some still exist in the wild, which explains why little is known about the mammal’s natural habits.

Tarsier

These primates are found on the islands of Samar, Leyte, Bohol, and Mindanao, as well as a handful of smaller islands like Dinagat and Basilan.

Tarsiers are a tourism mascot in Bohol, but there is little regulation surrounding this practice. Many tarsiers are exhibited in deplorable conditions at roadside attractions. Given the difficulty of keeping tarsiers alive in captivity, mortality is high among these creatures – when they die, replacements are seized from the wild. Deforestation and increased typhoons due to climate change also pose a threat to wild tarsiers.

Philippine Forest Turtle

The Philippines is home to five of seven sea turtle species in the world. All five of these species are threatened and listed as endangered.

The Philippine forest turtle, sought by collectors for its uniqueness, continues to face a major threat from the illicit pet trade. In 2015, a single bust in Palawan uncovered nearly 4,000 turtles.

The Crucial Role of Endangered Species in Maintaining Ecosystem Balance

Endangered species play a crucial role in maintaining the delicate balance of the ecosystem. Each species has its unique role in the food chain and its habitat, and the loss of any one of these species can have far-reaching consequences.

For example, predators play an important role in controlling the population of prey species, and herbivores play a critical role in maintaining the balance of vegetation in an ecosystem. Some species are responsible for pollination, which is essential for the growth of plants and the production of fruits and seeds. Others help to break down organic matter and recycle nutrients, contributing to soil health and supporting the growth of other organisms.

When an endangered species is lost, the ecosystem may suffer in a number of ways. The loss of a predator, for example, can lead to an overpopulation of prey species, which can cause overgrazing and damage to vegetation. The loss of a pollinator can lead to a decline in plant populations and reduce the availability of food and habitat for other species. The loss of a species that helps to recycle nutrients can cause a buildup of organic matter, which can lead to toxic conditions for other organisms.

In short, the loss of endangered species can have a ripple effect throughout the ecosystem, impacting the health and well-being of other species and ultimately affecting the functioning of the ecosystem as a whole. By working to protect and conserve endangered species, we can help to ensure that ecosystems remain healthy and resilient for generations to come.

Organizations Safeguarding Endangered Species in the Philippines

Tamaraw Conservation Program (TCP)

Established in 1979, the TCP focuses on conserving the critically endangered Tamaraw, a dwarf buffalo species found only on Mindoro Island. The program has included habitat protection, research, community engagement, and captive breeding efforts.

Philippine Eagle Foundation (PEF)

The PEF has been working to conserve the critically endangered Philippine Eagle since 1987. Activities include scientific research, captive breeding, habitat conservation, community-based conservation, and environmental education.

Haribon Foundation

Founded in 1972, Haribon Foundation is one of the Philippines' oldest environmental organizations. It has led efforts to conserve endangered species and their habitats through various initiatives, including the "Road to 2020" campaign, which aims to restore one million hectares of forest by 2020.

Save Philippine Seas (SPS)

This organization focuses on marine conservation and protecting endangered species like sharks, rays, and sea turtles. SPS has been involved in various activities, such as conducting research, organizing community-based conservation efforts, and launching awareness campaigns.

Mabuwaya Foundation

The Mabuwaya Foundation is dedicated to the conservation of the critically endangered Philippine crocodile. The organization has conducted research, established protected areas, and engaged local communities in conservation efforts.
